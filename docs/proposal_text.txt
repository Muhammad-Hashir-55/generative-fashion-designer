1. Abstract 
Pakistan's textile industry is one of the largest in the world, contributing over 60% of the country's total 
export earnings. Despite this, textile designers rely heavily on manual sketch -and-iterate workflows that 
are slow, expensive, and difficult to scale. This project proposes a Generative Fashion Designer system 
powered by fine -tuned diffusion models and neural style transfer to automate the rapid prototyping of 
traditional Pakistani textile patterns — including Ajrak, Khaddar, Phulkari, Sindhi block prints, and Balochi 
embroidery. Using prompt -based customization via CLIP text encoders and a curated domain -specific 
dataset, the system will enable fashion houses, SMEs, and e-commerce brands to generate high-fidelity, 
culturally authentic textile designs  on demand. The project directly integrates core DNN concepts — 
convolutional feature extraction, attention mechanisms, diffusion -based generation, and perceptual loss 
optimization — making it both academically rigorous and commercially viable. 
2. Problem Statement 
Pakistani textile designers face three critical bottlenecks in their current workflow:  
• Slow Prototyping: Creating a single new pattern design requires a skilled artisan 3-7 days of 
manual effort — from concept sketching to sample weaving. This makes rapid trend-responsive 
design economically infeasible for most SMEs. 
• Loss of Cultural Heritage: Many traditional patterns (Sindhi Rilli, Balochi mirror-work, Multani tile 
motifs) are undocumented and at risk of being lost as master artisans age without successors who 
can preserve the visual vocabulary. 
• No Scalable Customization Layer: Buyers and fashion houses cannot interactively request 
pattern variations (color palette swaps, motif density changes, fusion with modern aesthetics) 
without commissioning entirely new designs at high cost. 
These gaps represent an unmet need at the intersection of AI, design, and Pakistan's multi-billion dollar 
textile export economy.

4. Technical Architecture 
4.1 Dataset Construction 
A domain-specific dataset is central to the project's cultural authenticity. We will assemble a curated 
corpus from three sources: 
• Web scraping: High-resolution images of Pakistani textile patterns from commercial platforms 
(Khaadi, Gul Ahmed, Alkaram), government craft archives (Pakistan Craft Council), and museum 
databases. 
• Manual collection: Original photographs of artisan samples from local bazaars in Lahore, 
Karachi, and Hyderabad — covering Ajrak stamps, Sindhi Rilli patchwork, Phulkari embroidery, and 
Balochi mirrors. 
• Augmentation: Controlled augmentations — rotation, tiling, hue-shift within culturally plausible 
ranges — to expand the dataset without introducing non-authentic patterns. 
Each image will be paired with a structured text caption (e.g., 'Sindhi Ajrak geometric repeat pattern, 
deep indigo and madder red, block print style') for text-conditioned fine-tuning. 
4.2 Stable Diffusion Fine-tuning (Core DNN Component) 
The base model is Stable Diffusion v1.5 (or SDXL for higher resolution). Fine-tuning will use two 
complementary strategies: 
• DreamBooth: Subject-driven fine-tuning that teaches the model specific Pakistani pattern 
identifiers as unique tokens (e.g., ) so they can be reliably invoked at inference.  
• LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation): Lightweight adapter matrices injected into the UNet's cross-
attention layers, allowing efficient fine-tuning on limited GPU memory while preserving the base 
model's generalization capability. LoRA reduces trainable parameters by ~99% compared to full 
fine-tuning. 
Fine-tuned Stable Diffusion v1.5 / 
SDXL 
The UNet backbone consists of ResNet-style residual blocks interleaved with Transformer-based 
spatial attention, operating on a 64x64 latent space (decoded to 512x512 or 1024x1024 pixels via a 
VAE). 
Training objective: minimize the denoising score-matching loss on the curated dataset. 
4.3 Neural Style Transfer Module 
Style transfer serves as a post-processing and refinement layer. A user can provide a reference image of 
an existing textile (e.g., a scanned Ajrak swatch), and the system will transfer its visual texture and motif 
structure onto a freshly generated base pattern. 
Architecture: Pre-trained VGG-19 extracts content features (layers conv4_2) and style features (Gram 
matrices of conv1_1, conv2_1, conv3_1, conv4_1, conv5_1). Optimization minimizes the weighted sum 
of content loss and style loss via L-BFGS: 
L_total = α · L_content + β · L_style 
where α and β are content-style tradeoff weights tunable per design requirement. This gives designers 
fine-grained control: high α preserves the generated structure, high β imposes the reference texture more 
aggressively. 
4.4 Prompt-Based Customization via CLIP 
The user interaction layer is built around OpenAI's CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining) text 
encoder, which maps natural language descriptions into the same embedding space as visual features.  
This enables intuitive queries such as: 
• "Generate a Phulkari-style shawl pattern with golden and maroon threads on black silk" 
• "Create a geometric Sindhi tile repeat for men's khaddar kurta, earth tones" 
• "Blend Ajrak block-print motifs with contemporary minimalist white space" 
LoRA adapters bridge the CLIP text encoder's general -purpose embeddings to the fine -tuned domain 
vocabulary, ensuring that cultural pattern names in the prompt reliably activate the correct visual 
semantics in the diffusion UNet's cross-attention layers.
